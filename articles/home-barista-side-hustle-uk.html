Turning Good Coffee Into a Side Hustle in the UK

21 July 2026 · 5 min read · Checked July 2026

If you have got good at coffee at home, the natural next thought is whether it could earn. It can, but the honest routes are not the ones the dreamy videos show. Here is what actually makes money, what it takes, and the rules you cannot skip.

If you are still at the saving stage, our coffee cost breakdown covers that side first.

The rule before anything else

The moment you sell food or drink to the public, you are a food business. That means registering with your local council, for free, at least 28 days before you start, and following basic food hygiene. This is not optional and it is not hard, but skipping it can shut you down.

The routes that genuinely earn

Mobile coffee cart or bike. The classic. A converted cart or trike at markets, fairs, sports events and office parks. Startup is real money, a few thousand pounds for a decent setup, but a good pitch at a busy event can take hundreds in a day. Margins on coffee are strong, roughly 40 to 60p of cost in a £3 drink.

Market and event stalls. Lower commitment than a full cart. Rent a pitch at a weekend market, serve a tight menu, keep it simple. Stall fees of £15 to £40 a day change the maths, so pick busy locations.

Coffee content and teaching. If making coffee is the thing you love more than serving it, teaching it pays differently. Reviews, brewing guides, and beginner tutorials earn through affiliate links to the kit you recommend and, eventually, ads. It is slow, like all content, but it needs no food registration and no early mornings.

Selling beans or blends. Buying wholesale and selling your own labelled bags at markets or online. This is retail with its own labelling rules, but the margins can be good and it scales without you standing behind a machine all day.

What it realistically pays

A cart at a genuinely busy event can clear a few hundred pounds in a day after costs. A quiet pitch can barely cover the fee. The single biggest factor is footfall, so choosing the right events matters more than the coffee.

Content earns nothing for months, then a slow trickle from affiliate links on the gear you review. The winners build into a steady side income, most stay small.

The honest picture: the cart is real money but real early mornings and real upfront cost. The content is low cost and low commitment but slow. Pick the one that matches what you actually have, time or money.

Starting small and cheap

  1. Test the demand before you buy a cart. Offer coffee at a local car boot or community event with borrowed kit and a market stall setup.
  2. Register as a food business with your council first. Free, quick, required.
  3. Keep the menu tiny. Flat white, americano, one option each. Speed and consistency beat a long list.
  4. Track your takings per event so you learn which pitches are worth returning to.

The tax side

Coffee income is trading income. Past £1,000 of total sales in a tax year you register for Self Assessment, and your beans, cups and pitch fees are business expenses that reduce what you are taxed on. Full detail is in our side hustle tax guide.

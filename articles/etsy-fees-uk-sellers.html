Etsy Fees for UK Sellers: What You Really Pay Per Sale

21 July 2026 · 5 min read · Fees checked July 2026

Ask a new Etsy seller what the fees are and most will say six and a half percent. The real number on a typical UK sale is closer to fifteen percent once every charge is counted. Etsy does not hide these fees, but they sit in different places and most people never add them up.

This guide lists every fee a UK seller pays in 2026 and then runs one real sale through all of them, so you can price your products properly from day one.

The five fees every UK seller pays

Listing fee. Twenty US cents, roughly 16p, every time you list an item. The listing lasts four months. If the item does not sell and renews, you pay again. Multi quantity listings charge the fee again on each sale.

Transaction fee. 6.5% of the full amount the buyer pays. That includes postage and gift wrap, not just the item price. Charging £3.50 for delivery? Etsy takes 6.5% of that too.

Payment processing fee. For UK sellers this is 4% plus 20p per order through Etsy Payments, which is the only payment system available.

Regulatory operating fee. A UK specific charge on each order. From 22 June 2026 it rose from 0.32% to 0.48% of the order value.

VAT on the fees. If you are not VAT registered, Etsy adds 20% VAT on top of its own charges. Not on your sale, on the fees themselves. Most small sellers miss this one completely.

A worked example: the £25 candle

Say you sell a candle for £25 with £3.50 postage. The buyer pays £28.50. Here is what Etsy takes.

  1. Listing fee: £0.16
  2. Transaction fee, 6.5% of £28.50: £1.85
  3. Payment processing, 4% plus 20p: £1.34
  4. Regulatory operating fee, 0.48%: £0.14
  5. VAT at 20% on those fees: £0.70

Total: £4.19, which is 14.7% of what the buyer paid. You receive £24.31, and your postage, materials and time still have to come out of that. If your candle costs £6 to make and £3.20 to post, your actual profit is £15.11, not £25.

Price with that maths in front of you. A good rule: take the price you had in mind and check it survives losing 15% before costs.

Offsite ads: the fee you cannot always refuse

Etsy runs ads for your products on Google and social media. When a buyer clicks one and purchases within 30 days, you pay 15% of that sale, on top of everything above.

Under roughly £8,000 of sales in the last 12 months you can opt out in settings, and most new UK sellers should. Above that threshold the programme becomes mandatory, though the fee drops to 12%. Factor it in once your shop grows.

What actually sells from UK shops

The strongest UK categories stay consistent: personalised gifts, wedding stationery and signage, jewellery, prints, and digital downloads. Digital products deserve a hard look. No postage, no materials, no stock, and the same file sells hundreds of times. The fees above still apply, but nearly everything left over is profit.

Check what is already selling before you make anything. Search your product idea on Etsy, filter by UK shops, and look at review counts. Reviews are usually 20 to 30 times smaller than true sales numbers, so a listing with 500 reviews has likely sold over 10,000 units.

The tax side in one paragraph

Etsy reports UK seller earnings to HMRC once you pass 30 sales or roughly £1,700 in a year. And once your total sales pass £1,000 in a tax year, you need to register for Self Assessment. The full rules, including what the £1,000 trading allowance covers, are in our guide on side hustle tax rules in the UK.

Quick checklist before you open a shop

  1. Price so you keep at least 85% after Etsy fees, then subtract materials and postage to see real profit.
  2. Opt out of offsite ads while you are small.
  3. List in batches. Each 16p listing fee is small, but 100 listings is £16 before a single sale.
  4. Keep a simple record of every sale from day one. HMRC can already see your totals.

Best Cashback Credit Cards in the UK: What You Actually Earn

21 July 2026 · 5 min read · Rates checked July 2026

Cashback cards sound simple. Spend money, get a little back. But the headline rates in the adverts almost never match what lands in your account, because caps, fees and introductory periods change the maths completely.

This guide walks through the main UK options and shows you how to work out the real number for your own spending. Rates were checked in July 2026, but they change often, so always confirm on the provider's site before applying.

The rule that matters more than any rate

Pay the card off in full every month. Cashback cards charge high interest, often close to 30% APR. One month of interest can wipe out a full year of cashback. If you cannot clear the balance monthly, a cashback card will cost you money, not earn it.

Set up a direct debit for the full amount on day one. Then the rest of this guide is worth reading.

American Express: highest rates, one catch

Amex runs two main cashback cards.

The Cashback Everyday card has no annual fee. New cardholders get a boosted introductory rate for the first few months, capped at a set amount. After that it pays 0.5% on your first £10,000 of spending each year and 1% above that.

The paid Cashback card charges a small annual fee and pays 0.75% up to £10,000 and 1.25% beyond. The fee only makes sense if you spend heavily. Below roughly £10,000 a year, the free card usually wins.

The catch: not everywhere takes Amex. Most supermarkets and big chains do, but some small shops, pubs and tradespeople do not. If much of your spending goes to places that refuse Amex, your real earnings drop fast.

Santander Edge: steady but capped

The Santander Edge credit card pays 1% on all purchases, which is the strongest flat rate in the UK right now. Two things shrink it. Cashback is capped at £10 a month, so spending above £1,000 a month earns nothing extra. And the card carries a monthly fee that adds up to £48 a year.

Quick maths: hit the £10 cap every month and you earn £120, minus £48 in fees, leaving £72. Spend only £500 a month and you earn £60, minus £48, leaving £12. The cap and fee mean this card suits one narrow group: people who reliably spend close to £1,000 a month on a card.

The debit card route: Chase

Not a credit card, but worth knowing. The Chase current account pays 1% cashback on everyday debit card spending, with monthly caps and some account conditions. There is no credit check to worry about and no interest risk because you spend your own money.

For anyone who cannot get approved for a credit card yet, or does not trust themselves with one, this is the safest way to earn cashback in the UK.

How to pick in three steps

  1. Add up your card spending for a normal month. Not a guess, check your statements.
  2. For each card, multiply your yearly spend by the real rate, subtract any fee, and respect the caps. That single number is all that matters.
  3. Check acceptance. If your regular shops refuse Amex, cross it off no matter what the rate says.

A worked example. You spend £800 a month, £9,600 a year. Amex Cashback Everyday: £9,600 at 0.5% is £48, no fee. Santander Edge: £8 a month cashback is £96, minus £48 fee, leaving £48. A dead heat, and the decision comes down to whether your shops take Amex.

Two things people forget

Applying for a card leaves a mark on your credit file. Use an eligibility checker first, most providers and comparison sites offer one, it runs a soft check that lenders cannot see.

And cashback is not loyalty. Rates get cut, sometimes with little notice. Check your rate once a year and switch without guilt when a better card appears. The £20 you might gain takes ten minutes of admin.

How to Set Your Freelance Rate in the UK Without Undercharging

21 July 2026 · 6 min read · Checked July 2026

The most common freelance mistake in the UK is not charging too much. It is charging too little because you compared your rate to a salary and forgot everything a salary quietly pays for. Here is how to set a day rate that actually leaves you better off than a job.

Why your rate must beat your old salary, not match it

When you were employed, your employer paid for a lot you never saw: holiday, sick pay, pension contributions, National Insurance, equipment, and the days you were ill or between projects. As a freelancer, all of that comes out of your rate.

A £150 day rate is not the same as £150 a day in a job. From it you must cover the days you are not working, your own pension, your own time off, and your tax. Match your old salary and you will end up poorer for more risk.

The maths beginners skip

There are around 260 weekdays in a year. You will not bill all of them. Take off holiday, illness, admin days, marketing days, and the gaps between clients, and a realistic freelancer bills perhaps 150 to 200 days a year, not 260.

So work backwards. Decide the yearly income you actually need. Divide it by the days you will realistically bill, not the days that exist. Then add on tax, pension and a margin for the quiet months. The number that comes out is your floor, the rate below which freelancing is not worth it.

A simple worked example

Say you want £30,000 a year in your pocket. Add tax and National Insurance, a small pension, and a buffer, and you might need to bill around £45,000. Divide that by 180 billable days and your day rate needs to be about £250. Charge £150 because it sounds fair and you have quietly signed up to work harder than a job for less.

The costs to build in

  • Income tax and National Insurance, set aside from every payment, as in our tax guide.
  • A pension, because nobody is paying into one for you now.
  • Software, equipment, insurance and any tools of your trade.
  • Time off. Holiday and sick days you now fund yourself.
  • Unpaid work. Quotes, invoicing, chasing late payers, finding the next client.

How to raise your rate without fear

Set your rate in private, on paper, using the maths above, before a client ever asks. When they ask, state it plainly and do not apologise. The clients who leave over a fair rate were rarely the ones worth keeping.

Raise it for new clients first. You do not have to hike everyone at once. Quote new enquiries at the higher rate and let your average climb over time.

The honest bottom line

Undercharging feels safe and is the fastest way to burn out and quit. Do the maths on billable days, build in everything a salary used to hide, and price for the life you want, not the fear that you are asking for too much. You almost never are.

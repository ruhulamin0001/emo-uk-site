Grow Your Own in the UK: What Actually Cuts the Food Bill

21 July 2026 · 5 min read · Checked July 2026

Growing your own food to save money is half true. Some crops genuinely cut your bill. Others cost more in compost and effort than they ever return, and nobody tells beginners which is which.

Here is the honest split, so your first year actually saves money instead of quietly costing it.

The rule that decides what is worth growing

Grow the things that are expensive to buy and cheap to grow. Skip the things that are cheap in every supermarket.

A bag of potatoes costs almost nothing and takes up half your plot for months. Fresh herbs cost a fortune in little supermarket packets and grow like weeds on a windowsill. That contrast is the whole strategy.

What genuinely saves money

Herbs. The clearest win of all. A £3 supermarket packet of basil dies in days. A £1.50 seed packet or a £2 living pot on the windowsill gives you months. Parsley, mint, coriander, chives and rosemary all thrive with almost no attention.

Salad leaves. Bagged salad is expensive and rots fast. Cut and come again lettuce in a trough or grow bag gives you weeks of picking from one sowing, and it grows fast enough for an impatient beginner.

Tomatoes. A few plants in pots or a growbag produce far more than you would ever buy, and homegrown tomatoes taste like a different vegetable. High return for a small outlay.

Courgettes and runner beans. Prolific to the point of comedy. A couple of plants can feed a household and leave surplus, which is where our guide to selling garden produce comes in.

Chillies and spring onions. Both are pricey to buy, easy to grow, and take up almost no room.

What is usually not worth it for saving money

Main crop potatoes, onions and carrots are cheap in shops and take up a lot of space for a long time. Grow them because you enjoy it, not to save money.

Sweetcorn and cauliflower need space, time and good conditions for a small return. Fun, but not frugal.

Starting for under £30

  1. A few packets of seeds: herbs, salad, tomatoes. Around £8.
  2. A bag of peat free compost: about £6.
  3. Reused containers. Yoghurt pots for seedlings, an old trough or a £3 growbag for salad, a bucket with drainage holes for a tomato plant.
  4. A sunny windowsill or a doorstep. No garden required for herbs and salad.

That is a working setup for less than the cost of a couple of takeaways, and it produces food within weeks.

The three beginner mistakes

Sowing everything at once. Sow a little every couple of weeks so you get a steady supply instead of a glut then nothing.

Forgetting to water pots. Containers dry out fast in summer. A daily check in July is the difference between a harvest and a graveyard.

Buying too much kit. You do not need a greenhouse, raised beds and gadgets in year one. Seeds, compost, containers and a windowsill prove whether you enjoy it before you spend real money.

The honest saving

Grow herbs and salad well and you can genuinely knock a few pounds a week off the shop, which is £100 to £200 over a season, from a £30 start. It is not going to clear your mortgage, but few habits pay back that fast, and the food is fresher than anything you can buy.

Web Hosting in the UK for Beginners: What You Actually Pay

21 July 2026 · 6 min read · Checked July 2026

You want a website. Every host tells you it is fast, secure and cheap, and the prices seem tiny. Then the renewal arrives and it has trebled. Here is the beginner honest version of what web hosting is, what you should pay, and the one trick that catches almost everyone.

What hosting actually is

A website is just files sitting on a computer that is always switched on and connected to the internet. Hosting is renting space on that computer. A domain name, like the address people type, is separate and usually cheap, around £10 a year.

The types, in plain words

Shared hosting. Your site sits on a machine with many others. Cheapest, slowest under heavy load, and completely fine for a new site, a blog or a small business. This is where almost everyone should start.

Managed or platform hosting. The host handles the technical upkeep for you, at a higher monthly price. Worth it later, when your site earns and downtime costs you money.

The expensive tiers, dedicated and cloud setups. You do not need these as a beginner. If someone is selling you one for your first blog, walk away.

The renewal price trick

This is the part to burn into your memory. The headline price, that lovely couple of pounds a month, is almost always an introductory rate for the first term only. When it renews, often after a year or more, it jumps to the real price, which can be two or three times higher.

Before you buy, find the renewal price, not the offer price. It is in the small print on purpose. Judge the host on what it costs in year two, because that is what you will actually pay for most of the time you use it.

What a beginner should really spend

  1. A domain name, roughly £8 to £12 a year.
  2. Shared hosting, honestly a few pounds a month once you look past the intro rate.
  3. A free SSL certificate, the little padlock, which good hosts now include at no cost. Never pay extra for this.

That is it. A brand new UK website should cost you the price of a couple of coffees a month, not hundreds a year.

The free option, and its catch

Free hosting exists and is fine for learning or a hobby page. The catch is you usually cannot use your own domain properly, the address looks unprofessional, and the host can show its own adverts on your site. For anything you want taken seriously, pay the few pounds.

Before you commit

Check the renewal price, check that SSL is included free, check there is a refund window, and check you can move your domain away later if you want to. A host that makes leaving hard is a host to avoid. Keep your login details in a proper vault, as covered in our password manager guide.

The honest bottom line

Hosting is one of the few genuinely cheap parts of running a website. The only real trap is the renewal jump. Know that price before you pay, start on shared hosting, and put your effort into what goes on the site rather than the machine underneath it.

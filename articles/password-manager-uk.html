Password Managers: The Free One Is Probably Enough

21 July 2026 · 5 min read · Prices checked July 2026

Most people run their whole financial life on three passwords and a variation of their dog's name. Banking, email, Amazon, the lot.

That is the real risk, and it is worth fixing, but not by spending money you do not need to spend. The good news is that the best free password manager is better than most people's paid setup. Here is what actually matters.

Why this is a money problem, not just a tech one

Reusing one password everywhere means a single leak from a shopping site you forgot about hands someone the key to your email. From your email they can reset everything else, including anything with your card on it.

That is how most account takeovers happen in the UK. Not clever hacking, just an old password from a leaked database being tried everywhere until something opens.

A password manager fixes this in one move: every site gets a different long password, and you only remember one.

What the free versions actually give you

This is the part the review sites bury, because free products pay no commission.

Bitwarden's free plan stores unlimited passwords across unlimited devices, syncs everywhere, and is open source with public security audits. For one person, this is genuinely all you need, forever, at no cost.

Proton Pass has a strong free tier too, from the same company as Proton VPN and Proton Mail, useful if you already use their other services.

Apple and Google both have password tools built into your phone and browser at no cost. They work well if your whole household lives on one ecosystem, and badly the moment someone has an iPhone and a Windows laptop.

When paying is genuinely worth it

Family sharing. Paid family plans let a household share passwords properly instead of texting each other the Netflix login. Bitwarden's family plan covers six people for around £40 a year, roughly 55p per person per month. That is the best value in the category.

Polish and support. 1Password costs more, around £48 a year for one person, and has no free tier. What you get is the smoothest experience and the best support. Some people happily pay for that, and it is a reasonable choice, just not a necessary one.

Business use. If you run a side hustle with a virtual assistant or a partner, shared vaults with proper permissions are worth the small cost, and it is a legitimate business expense.

The one rule that matters more than the brand

Your master password is the whole system. Make it long, make it unique, and never use it anywhere else.

Three or four unrelated words strung together beats a short jumble of symbols, because length is what defeats guessing. Write it down on paper and keep it somewhere safe at home. That advice sounds wrong and it is correct: a burglar in your house is a smaller risk than a data breach on the other side of the world.

Turn on two factor authentication for the password manager itself. That way a stolen master password alone is not enough.

Switching without the pain

  1. Install the free one, add the browser extension.
  2. Do not import everything at once. Let it capture logins as you use sites normally over two weeks.
  3. Change the important ones first: email, banking, anything with a saved card. Those four or five accounts are 90% of the risk.
  4. Once your email password is unique and strong, the rest can wait.

The honest summary

Install Bitwarden free. If your household needs shared access, upgrade to the family plan for about £40 a year. If you want the most polished product and do not mind £48 a year for one person, 1Password is good and nobody should tell you otherwise.

What matters is that you stop using the same password everywhere, and the free tool does that just as well as the expensive one. If you also want to know whether the other security subscription is worth it, we covered that in our guide to VPNs in the UK.

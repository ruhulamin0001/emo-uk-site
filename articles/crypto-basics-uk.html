Crypto for Beginners in the UK: The Rules, the Tax, the Risks

21 July 2026 · 6 min read · Rules checked July 2026

Crypto is loud. Everyone either got rich or lost everything, and both stories are usually exaggerated. This is the quiet version for a UK beginner, focused on the parts that actually matter: the tax, the rules, and how not to lose money to the obvious traps.

This is information, not advice. Nobody here is telling you to buy anything.

What crypto actually is, in one paragraph

A cryptocurrency is a digital asset recorded on a shared public ledger rather than held by a bank. Bitcoin is the original. There are thousands of others, most of which will not exist in a few years. Its price can move violently in both directions, which is the whole reason people are excited and the whole reason people get hurt.

How the UK taxes it

HMRC treats crypto as property, not currency. That has clear consequences.

Simply holding crypto is not taxed. You can buy it and hold it for years and owe nothing while you do.

Selling, swapping one coin for another, or spending it counts as a disposal and can trigger Capital Gains Tax on the profit. There is a tax free allowance, £3,000 for the 2024/25 year, and gains above that are taxed at your capital gains rate.

Earning crypto, for example through some staking or mining, can be treated as income and taxed accordingly.

From 2026, UK crypto exchanges must report user data to HMRC under new international rules. The days of assuming HMRC cannot see your account are over. If you make gains above the allowance, declare them through Self Assessment, the same system covered in our side hustle tax guide.

What the FCA does and does not protect

This is the part beginners misunderstand and it is the most important part.

Most crypto is not covered by the Financial Services Compensation Scheme. If a bank fails, your money is protected up to £85,000. If a crypto exchange collapses or your coins vanish, there is usually no such safety net. That protection you rely on everywhere else does not apply here.

The FCA regulates how crypto is promoted in the UK and bans certain incentives, but it does not guarantee you will get your money back. Treat every pound in crypto as money you could lose entirely, because you could.

The traps that cost beginners the most

Putting in money you cannot afford to lose. The single biggest mistake. Rent, bills and emergency savings must never go near crypto.

Chasing a coin that already went up. By the time a coin is all over social media, the easy gains are usually gone and you are buying at the top.

Scams and fake giveaways. No legitimate person will double your crypto if you send them some first. Anyone promising guaranteed returns is lying. A celebrity endorsement in an advert is almost always fake.

Losing your own keys. If you hold crypto in your own wallet and lose the recovery phrase, it is gone forever, with nobody to call. Write the phrase on paper and store it safely, the same logic as in our password manager guide.

If you still want to start

  1. Only use money you could lose without it changing your life.
  2. Use an established, FCA registered exchange rather than a random app from an advert.
  3. Start tiny. A small amount teaches you the mechanics without a large risk.
  4. Keep records of every buy and sell from day one, because you will need them at tax time.
  5. Turn on two factor authentication and never share your recovery phrase with anyone, ever.

The honest bottom line

Crypto is a high risk, speculative asset, not a savings account and not a side hustle with predictable pay. Some people do well. Many lose money. Nobody who is honest can tell you which you will be.

If your goal is a reliable extra income rather than a gamble, the other guides on this site, like print on demand or remote work, are far more predictable places to put your effort.

Selling Your Garden Produce in the UK: The Rules and the Money

21 July 2026 · 6 min read · Rules checked July 2026

Every summer the same thing happens. Six courgette plants seemed sensible in April, and by August you are leaving bags of them on neighbours' doorsteps.

Selling the surplus is allowed in the UK, and plenty of people do it from a table at the front gate. The rules are simpler than the internet suggests, but they change sharply depending on what you sell. Here is the honest version, and what the money actually looks like.

The one distinction that decides everything

UK food rules split what you grow into two groups.

Primary products are unprocessed things straight from the ground or the bird: vegetables, fruit, herbs, eggs. Selling these in small amounts to your local community is the lightest touch situation there is.

Processed products are anything you have made: jam, chutney, pickles, cakes, sauces. The moment you cook, bottle or preserve something, more rules apply, because you have moved from selling a carrot to running a small food operation.

Know which group you are in before you do anything else.

Selling veg and fruit

This is the easy end. A table at the gate with a few boxes of runner beans and an honesty box is normal practice across the country.

Keep it sensible: sell it clean, sell it fresh, and do not make health claims. If your plot is an allotment, check your tenancy agreement first, because many councils restrict or forbid selling produce grown on council land. That single check saves a lot of trouble.

Realistic money: a decent vegetable plot in a good year might produce £200 to £400 of surplus. It is pocket money, not a business, and the real return is that you stop buying vegetables yourself.

Selling eggs

Eggs have their own specific rules, and people get caught out here.

With a small flock, under 50 birds, you can sell ungraded eggs directly to consumers at the farm gate or door, not through shops. Selling loose ungraded eggs from your own premises, including via an honesty box, normally means registering as a food business with your council. Registration is free and straightforward, it is not a licence or an inspection fee.

Two dates matter. The best before date must be no more than 28 days from laying, and eggs must reach the customer within 21 days of laying. Do not wash them, and do not sell cracked ones.

Realistic money: half a dozen sells for around £2 to £3.50 depending on area. Six hens laying well might bring in £15 to £25 a month in summer, against feed costs. This is a hobby that partly pays for itself, not an income.

Selling jam, chutney and baked goods

Here the bar rises. You need to register as a food business with your local council, which is free and must be done at least 28 days before you start trading.

You will also need to label properly, which means the name of the food, the full ingredients list, allergens emphasised, a date mark, weight, and your business name and address. Allergen labelling is the part people skip and it is the part that matters most.

Your kitchen can usually be your home kitchen. Environmental Health may visit, but a clean, organised domestic kitchen with basic records normally passes. The council would rather help you get it right than shut you down.

Realistic money: jars sell for £3.50 to £6 at markets and fetes. Ingredients and jars cost roughly £1 to £2 a jar. It becomes real money only at volume, which means market stalls, and stall fees of £15 to £40 a day change the maths quickly.

Where to actually sell

Gate stall with an honesty box: no fees, no travel, works well on a road with foot traffic. Expect some loss and accept it.

Local community groups on Facebook: free, and where most people now find local produce. Post photos in the morning when you pick.

Village markets and fetes: better prices and volume, but a stall fee and a whole day of your time. Do the maths before committing.

Farm shops and cafes: they buy in bulk at lower prices but take everything, which suits a glut.

The tax bit, briefly

Money from selling produce counts as trading income. The first £1,000 of total sales in a tax year is covered by the trading allowance, so most gate sellers never need to tell HMRC anything.

Past £1,000 in gross sales, not profit, you need to register for Self Assessment. The full explanation is in our side hustle tax rules guide.

Is it worth doing

As an income, no. As a way to stop wasting a glut, cover your seed and feed costs, and meet your neighbours, it is one of the better things you can do with a garden.

If you want the growing to actually pay, the money is usually in the next step: seedlings and plants in spring sell far better than vegetables in summer, and preserves sell better than the fruit they came from. Value added beats volume, the same lesson as our print on demand guide.

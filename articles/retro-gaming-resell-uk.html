Flipping Retro Games in the UK: Where the Real Money Is

21 July 2026 · 6 min read · Checked July 2026

Old games sell for surprising money, and the videos make it look like every car boot has a hidden fortune. Some of that is true. Most of it is not. Here is how retro game reselling actually works in the UK, where the profit really sits, and the traps that catch new flippers.

What actually sells for money

Complete in box beats loose every time. A cartridge alone is worth a fraction of the same game with its box and manual. Condition and completeness are most of the value.

Certain systems and titles hold value strongly: boxed titles for classic consoles, sought after handhelds, and games that never got a big reprint. Common sports titles and the games everyone owned are worth little, no matter how old.

Consoles themselves, especially boxed and working with all the cables, sell steadily. A tested, complete console is easier money than gambling on rare cartridges.

Where to buy below value

  • Car boot sales, early. The best stock is gone by mid morning.
  • House clearances and local selling apps, where sellers often just want it gone.
  • Charity shops, which increasingly price games well, but not always.
  • Job lots. A messy box of mixed games can hide one item that covers the whole cost.

The fakes problem

Reproduction cartridges are everywhere and getting better. A fake sold as genuine will get you a return, a bad rating, and possibly a ban from the platform. Learn the signs: the wrong label finish, the wrong board inside, a back sticker that looks freshly printed. If a rare game is priced too well to be true, assume it is a copy until you can prove otherwise.

The honest profit after fees

Say you buy a boxed game for £8 at a boot sale and sell it for £35. Sounds like £27 profit. Now take off the selling platform fee, the postage, the packaging, and the time spent listing and posting. The real profit might be £18. Still good, but not the £27 the headline suggested.

The money is real, but it is made on volume and knowledge, not on one lucky find. The flippers who earn steadily know exactly what a title is worth before they buy it.

The tax and selling rules

Buying specifically to resell is trading. Once your total sales pass £1,000 in a tax year, you register for Self Assessment, as set out in our side hustle tax guide. This is different from selling off your own old collection, which is usually not taxed. Keep records of what you paid and what you sold from the start.

Starting sensibly

  1. Pick one or two systems and learn their prices deeply before you spend real money.
  2. Buy complete, working, and honest. Test everything you can.
  3. Photograph well and describe flaws plainly. Returns eat your profit fastest.
  4. Track every buy and sell so you know your true margin, not the headline one.

The honest bottom line

Retro game flipping is a genuine UK side hustle for someone who enjoys the hunt and learns the values. It rewards patience and knowledge, not luck. Treat it as a slow build and it can pay well. Chase the one big find and you will mostly buy other people's junk.

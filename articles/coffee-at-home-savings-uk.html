Coffee at Home: How Much a Daily Flat White Really Costs You

21 July 2026 · 5 min read · Prices checked July 2026

You have heard the advice before. Stop buying coffee and you will be rich. It is usually said by someone who has never worked a shift that needed a coffee to survive.

So here is the version with actual numbers, no lecture. What one daily coffee costs across a year, what the same drink costs at home, and what kit is worth buying before the maths stops working.

What you pay outside

A small flat white at Costa runs about £4.35 in 2026. Starbucks sits slightly higher, Greggs sits far lower at around £1.85, and independents vary by city. Prices in London are noticeably above the rest of the country.

Take the middle ground. One £4 coffee on each of your working days, say 250 days a year, comes to £1,000. Buy one every day of the year and it is £1,460.

That number surprises people because £4 never feels like a decision. It feels like nothing, roughly 240 times a year.

What the same drink costs in your kitchen

Supermarket beans cost around £5 to £7 for a 250g bag. That bag makes about 30 espressos, which works out at roughly 20p of coffee per drink. Milk for a flat white adds about 15p. Call it 35 to 40p a cup, and that is with decent beans, not the cheapest on the shelf.

So the same drink is around £4 outside and around 40p at home. On 250 working days that is £1,000 against £100.

The gap is roughly £900 a year, and that is before you count the sandwich you buy because you were already standing in the queue.

What the kit costs, honestly

The savings only work if the machine is not silly money. Three routes, cheapest first.

Cafetiere or French press, £10 to £20. Makes good black coffee, no electricity beyond the kettle, nothing to break. If you drink your coffee black this is the whole answer and you can stop reading.

Moka pot or AeroPress, £20 to £35. Both make something close to espresso strength. Add a £10 handheld milk frother and you have a flat white that is genuinely close to the shop version. This is the sweet spot for most people. Total setup under £50, paid back in under three weeks.

Bean to cup machine, £200 to £500. Real espresso, grinder built in, one button. Payback takes two to five months at a coffee a day, which is fine if you will keep using it. The graveyard of unused coffee machines exists for a reason, so buy this one after a few months of the cheap route, not before.

Skip pod machines if saving money is the goal. Pods work out around 30 to 40p each, similar to beans, but you are locked into one brand and paying a premium forever on the refills.

The trap that ruins the maths

Buying £11 speciality beans every week undoes the whole exercise. Speciality coffee is a hobby, and a good one, but it is not a saving. If the goal is money, a £6 supermarket bag makes coffee that is better than most chains anyway, because it will be fresher than beans sitting in a hopper all day.

Watch the milk too. A daily oat milk habit at £2 a carton adds up faster than the coffee does.

A realistic plan

  1. Buy an AeroPress or moka pot and a handheld frother, under £50 total.
  2. Pick one supermarket bean at £5 to £7 and stick with it for a month.
  3. Keep one bought coffee a week as a treat, not zero. Total bans fail.
  4. Move the difference into savings the same day each month, so it becomes real money rather than money that quietly disappears.

Even keeping one shop coffee a week, you would spend around £300 a year instead of £1,000. That is £700 kept, from a change that takes four minutes each morning.

Where the saved money could go

£700 a year sitting in a current account earns nothing. In an easy access savings account it earns a little. Put toward a card balance charging 25% interest, it is worth far more than any savings rate, which is covered in our guide to UK cashback credit cards.

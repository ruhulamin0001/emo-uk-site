Print on Demand UK: The Real Profit Per T-Shirt

21 July 2026 · 5 min read · Costs checked July 2026

Print on demand looks like the perfect side hustle. No stock, no printing machine, no packing. You upload a design, someone buys, a factory prints and posts it, and you keep the difference.

The catch is in the size of that difference. On a £25 t-shirt most UK sellers keep between £4 and £8, not the £15 the videos suggest. Here is the full maths so you can decide with real numbers.

What you actually pay per shirt

Two suppliers dominate: Printful and Printify. Both print in the UK or EU for UK orders, which keeps delivery times reasonable.

Base cost for a decent quality t-shirt sits around £9 to £11 with Printful. Printify is usually cheaper, often around £7 to £9 for a similar Bella and Canvas shirt, and cheaper still on their paid plan. Printify uses a network of separate print shops, so quality varies by which one fills your order. Printful prints in its own factories, which costs more but stays consistent.

Shipping is the fee people forget. Expect roughly £4 to £5 for a single shirt to a UK address. It drops per item when someone buys two or three together, which is why bundle offers matter.

So before you have sold anything, one shirt costs you somewhere between £12 and £16 landed.

The £25 t-shirt, all the way through

Say you sell on Etsy for £25 with free delivery built into the price.

  1. Supplier base cost: £10.00
  2. Supplier shipping to your buyer: £4.50
  3. Etsy listing fee: £0.16
  4. Etsy transaction fee, 6.5%: £1.63
  5. Etsy payment processing, 4% plus 20p: £1.20
  6. VAT on the Etsy fees: £0.60

Total out: £18.09. You keep £6.91 on a £25 sale, which is 27.6%.

That is a real profit, but notice what it is not. It is not passive. It is £6.91 for the design work, the listing, the customer questions, and the returns. Sell ten shirts a month and you have made £69.

The three ways to make the numbers work

Raise the price, not the volume. A £32 shirt with the same costs leaves £11.50 instead of £6.91. Buyers who want a specific design rarely price compare the way they do for plain basics. Niche designs support higher prices, generic slogans do not.

Sell products with better margins. Mugs, tote bags, posters and stickers often cost £4 to £7 to make and sell for £15 to £20. The percentage kept is usually better than apparel, and returns are rarer because sizing never goes wrong.

Own the customer, not just the sale. Selling from your own Shopify or Etsy shop with repeat buyers beats chasing one off sales. The second sale to the same person costs you nothing in marketing.

Where UK sellers lose money

Sizing returns. Apparel returns run high because sizing charts confuse people. Print on demand suppliers will not usually refund you for a customer who ordered the wrong size, so that cost lands on you. Put exact chest measurements in every listing, not just S, M and L.

Cheap tests. Ordering samples feels like a waste of money until you send out fifty shirts with a print that cracks after two washes. Order one of everything you plan to sell and wash it three times before listing it.

Copying trends. Slogans and characters from films, football clubs and brands get listings removed and shops closed. Original designs are slower to sell and far safer.

The tax and admin side

Print on demand income counts as trading income. Once your total sales pass £1,000 in a tax year you need to register for Self Assessment. Full detail is in our side hustle tax rules guide.

Keep every supplier invoice. Base costs and shipping you pay are business expenses, so they reduce the profit you are taxed on. A folder and a simple spreadsheet is enough at this stage.

Is it worth starting

Yes, if you treat it as a design business rather than a shortcut. The printing is solved for you. What is left is the hard part, which is making something people actually want to wear, and that is also the part nobody can automate away from you.

Start with five designs in one clear niche, order samples, price for at least £8 profit, and see what sells before you make fifty more.

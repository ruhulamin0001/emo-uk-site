DIY Home Jobs That Save Real Money (and Ones to Never Attempt)

21 July 2026 · 6 min read · Checked July 2026

A tradesperson's call out fee alone can be £60 to £100 before they lift a tool. For simple jobs, that is the whole cost, and it is why learning a handful of basic tasks saves more than almost any coupon.

Here is the honest split: what to do yourself, what to pay for, and the two things you must never touch by law.

Jobs a beginner can safely do

Painting and decorating. The biggest easy win. Decorators charge largely for time, and a room you paint yourself over a weekend saves hundreds. Preparation is 90% of the result, so fill, sand and use masking tape properly.

Filling cracks and holes. A £5 tub of filler and a scraper fixes what looks like a job for a plasterer, as long as it is cosmetic and small.

Bleeding radiators, changing tap washers, silicone sealing a bath. All simple, all things people pay £80 call outs for, all learnable from a ten minute video.

Flat pack furniture and putting up shelves. Get a cheap spirit level and a stud finder and you can do these confidently.

Draught proofing. Cheap strips around doors and windows cost a few pounds and cut heating bills every winter after. One of the best value jobs on this list.

Jobs where paying a pro is the cheaper choice

Plastering a whole wall. It looks easy and it is not. A botched wall costs more to fix than to have done right first time.

Tiling a floor or a wet area. Small splashbacks are fine for a careful beginner. Bathrooms and floors, where water getting behind the tiles causes expensive damage, are worth a professional.

Anything structural. Removing a wall, touching joists, altering a roof. The risk is not the labour cost, it is the building coming to harm.

The two you must never do yourself

Gas work. It is illegal in the UK to do gas work unless you are Gas Safe registered. Boilers, gas hobs, gas fires, all of it. This is not a guideline, it is the law, and for good reason.

Notifiable electrical work. Major electrical jobs must be done or signed off by a qualified, registered electrician under the building regulations. Changing a plug or a light fitting is usually fine, but new circuits, consumer units and bathroom wiring are not DIY territory.

On both of these, doing it yourself does not just risk a fine. It risks your home insurance being void if something goes wrong, which turns a saved £100 into a catastrophe.

The starter toolkit

  1. A cordless drill and driver. The one tool that changes everything.
  2. A basic hand tool set: screwdrivers, hammer, pliers, tape measure.
  3. A spirit level and a stud and cable detector.
  4. Filler, sandpaper, masking tape and a decent paintbrush.

That kit costs less than a single tradesperson visit and pays for itself on the first job.

The realistic saving

Do your own decorating, small repairs and draught proofing across a year and you easily save several hundred pounds against calling someone for each. Draught proofing alone keeps saving on heating every winter after.

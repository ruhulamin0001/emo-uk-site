Remote Jobs UK With No Degree: What Is Real and What Pays

21 July 2026 · 5 min read · Checked July 2026

Search for work from home jobs in the UK and you will drown in noise. Half the results promise £500 a day for two hours of typing. Those are not jobs.

Real remote work without a degree does exist, thousands of people do it, and the pay is ordinary rather than magic. Here is what those roles actually are, what they pay, and how to tell a real listing from a trap.

The roles that genuinely hire without a degree

Customer service is the biggest category by a distance. Amazon, BT, Virgin Media, Apple and dozens of retailers and utilities run home based advisor teams handling calls, email and live chat. Most ask for GCSE level English and maths, decent broadband and a quiet room. Not a degree.

Pay usually starts around £10 to £12 an hour, with extra for evenings and weekends. Training is normally two to six weeks and, in the real ones, paid.

Admin and data entry roles sit alongside this. Fewer of them, more competition, similar money. Be careful here, because this is also the category scammers imitate most.

Virtual assistant work pays a little better once you have a few months of experience, often £12 to £18 an hour, because you are handling inboxes, diaries and simple bookkeeping rather than following a script.

What the money really looks like

At £11 an hour, full time is about £21,000 a year before tax. It is not a fortune and nobody should pretend otherwise. What it does give you is no commute, which quietly saves £1,000 to £2,000 a year and an hour or more each day.

The more useful way to see these jobs is as a doorway. A year on a support desk teaches systems, tone, and how a company handles customers. That experience opens support team leader, onboarding, and account management roles, and those pay considerably more.

How to spot the scams

Four rules keep you safe.

  1. A real employer never asks you to pay. No training fee, no starter kit, no admin charge. If money moves from you to them, walk away.
  2. Never accept a job that asks you to receive payments and forward them on. That is money laundering, and the person doing the forwarding is the one who gets prosecuted.
  3. Be wary of interviews conducted entirely over messaging apps with no video and no company email address.
  4. Check the company on Companies House. It takes thirty seconds and tells you whether the business exists.

Legitimate remote roles also do background checks, ask for references, and send a real contract. Scams skip all of that and rush you.

Where to look

Indeed and Glassdoor carry the volume, and both let you filter by remote and by no experience needed. Search directly on the careers pages of large employers too, because their own site often lists home based roles before the aggregators pick them up.

One practical tip that beats any job board trick: apply early. Remote listings attract hundreds of applications within days. A CV sent on day one gets read. The same CV on day six often does not.

Getting picked when you have no experience

Rewrite your CV for the specific role rather than sending the same one everywhere. If the advert says empathy, patience and accuracy, those exact words should appear in your CV where they are true.

Mention any customer facing work you have done, even retail, hospitality or volunteering. Dealing with an angry customer at a till is the same skill as dealing with one on a webchat.

Say plainly that you have a suitable home setup: reliable broadband, a quiet space, and a computer. Employers screen for this and most applicants forget to mention it.

Worth knowing before you start

Employed remote work is taxed through PAYE like any job, so there is nothing extra to do. If instead you go self employed as a virtual assistant or freelancer, different rules apply once your income passes £1,000, and those are covered in our side hustle tax rules guide.

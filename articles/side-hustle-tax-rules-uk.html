Side Hustle Tax Rules in the UK: When HMRC Needs to Know

21 July 2026 · 5 min read · Facts checked July 2026

You sell a few things on Vinted. You do some freelance work at weekends. Maybe you walk dogs for neighbours. At some point one question shows up. Do I need to tell the taxman?

The rules are simpler than most people think. This guide covers the three numbers that matter, what online platforms now share with HMRC, and the one situation where you owe nothing at all.

The £1,000 rule is the starting point

Every person in the UK gets a trading allowance of £1,000 per tax year. The tax year runs from 6 April to 5 April.

If your total side income stays at £1,000 or below, you owe no tax on it and you do not need to tell HMRC anything. No forms, no registration, nothing.

Two details catch people out.

First, the £1,000 means your total sales, not your profit. If you sold £1,200 worth of candles and spent £500 on wax and jars, you are over the limit. Your sales were £1,200.

Second, all your side hustles share one allowance. Freelance work, market stalls, tutoring, online selling, it all adds up together. You do not get £1,000 per activity.

Over £1,000: register for Self Assessment

Once your gross side income passes £1,000 in a tax year, you need to register for Self Assessment with HMRC. You can do this on the GOV.UK website in about ten minutes. You will need a Government Gateway account and your National Insurance number.

The deadline to register is 5 October after the end of the tax year in which you passed the limit. So if you crossed £1,000 during the year ending 5 April 2026, you must register by 5 October 2026.

Registering does not always mean paying. If your profit is small and your total income sits under your Personal Allowance of £12,570, you may owe nothing. But the return still has to be filed.

The £3,000 change everyone talks about

You may have seen headlines saying the threshold is rising to £3,000. Here is the accurate version.

The government has announced a plan to raise the reporting threshold from £1,000 to £3,000. When it takes effect, people earning between £1,000 and £3,000 would use a simple online service instead of a full tax return. Around 300,000 people would stop filing returns.

Two things to note. The change is not yet law, so the £1,000 rule still applies right now. And the tax free amount stays at £1,000 either way. Between £1,000 and £3,000 you may still owe tax, you would just report it in a simpler way.

Check the current status on GOV.UK before you rely on it.

What Vinted, eBay and Airbnb now tell HMRC

Since January 2024, digital platforms in the UK must report seller information to HMRC. This covers eBay, Vinted, Etsy, Depop, Airbnb, Uber, Deliveroo and similar sites.

A platform reports you when you pass either of these in a calendar year: 30 sales, or roughly £1,700 in earnings (the rule is set at 2,000 euros). They send your name, address, total earnings and transaction count. Reports go in by 31 January for the previous year.

This is not a new tax. It is a visibility tool. But it means HMRC can now compare what platforms say you earned against what you declared. If those numbers do not match, expect a letter.

Selling your own old stuff is usually not taxable

This is the part that causes the most needless panic.

Clearing out your wardrobe, selling your old phone, or listing unwanted gifts on Vinted is not trading. You are selling personal possessions, usually for less than you paid. There is no tax on that, no matter the amount, and the £1,000 limit is not relevant.

Trading means you buy things to resell, or you make things to sell, with the aim of profit. Buying ten pairs of trainers to flip is trading. Selling your own trainers because they no longer fit is not.

One exception exists for single valuable items. If you sell a personal possession for more than £6,000, such as a painting or jewellery, Capital Gains Tax rules can apply. For everyday clearouts this almost never comes up.

A quick self check

Ask yourself three questions.

  1. Am I selling my own used possessions? If yes, relax. No tax, no forms.
  2. Am I making or buying things to sell, or doing paid work on the side? If yes, add up your total sales for the tax year.
  3. Is that total over £1,000? If yes, register for Self Assessment by 5 October. If no, you are done.

Keep simple records either way. A spreadsheet with dates, amounts and costs takes minutes to maintain and settles any question HMRC ever asks.
